# Episode 3: How Much Can You Earn? Think in Percentages, Not Rupees

Most beginners enter the stock market with the wrong question:

"How much money can I make?"

A professional trader asks:

"What percentage return can I realistically generate?"

This small shift changes everything.

---

## The Two Beginner Mindsets

### Case 1: The Monthly Expense Trader

Suppose Rahul from Guntur has monthly expenses of ₹50,000.

He thinks:

"I need ₹50,000 every month.

There are around 20 trading days in a month.

So I just need ₹2,500 profit every day."

Sounds simple.

But there is a hidden problem.

Rahul never asked:

"How much capital do I need to safely generate ₹2,500 every day?"

---

### The Reality Check

Suppose Rahul starts with ₹50,000.

To earn ₹2,500 daily:

₹2,500 ÷ ₹50,000 × 100 = 5%

He needs a 5% return every single day.

If he trades 20 days a month:

5% × 20 = 100% per month

That means he expects his money to double every month.

₹50,000 → ₹1,00,000 → ₹2,00,000 → ₹4,00,000

Within a year he would become a multi-crore trader.

If it were that easy, everyone would be rich.

This is why percentage thinking matters.

---

### Case 2: The Random Trader

Another beginner says:

"I have ₹50,000.

Whatever profit comes, comes."

Sometimes ₹500.

Sometimes ₹5,000.

Sometimes ₹10,000.

This is also dangerous.

Why?

Because there is no system.

No target.

No risk control.

No business plan.

This is gambling, not trading.

---

## Think Like a Business Owner

Imagine you open a tea shop.

Investment = ₹2 lakh

At year-end, you make ₹40,000 profit.

Would you say:

"I made ₹40,000."

No.

A business owner says:

"I generated 20% return on capital."

The stock market should be treated exactly the same way.

Your trading account is your business.

---

## The ₹10,000 Monthly Profit Illusion

Many beginners think:

"I only need ₹10,000 extra every month."

Sounds small.

Let's calculate.

Capital = ₹50,000

Desired monthly profit = ₹10,000

₹10,000 ÷ ₹50,000 × 100 = 20%

You need 20% every month.

Annual equivalent:

20% × 12 = 240%

You are expecting your money to more than triple every year.

Even the world's best investors struggle to achieve such returns consistently.

Now you can see why percentage thinking exposes unrealistic expectations.

---

## How Much Capital Do You Actually Need?

Technically you can buy shares with:

* ₹5,000
* ₹10,000
* ₹20,000

But surviving in markets is different from placing a trade.

Suppose you have only ₹20,000.

You take three bad trades.

Losses:

₹3,000
₹2,500
₹4,000

Suddenly your capital becomes:

₹10,500

Now emotions take over.

Fear increases.

Confidence drops.

One more bad trade can destroy the account.

This is why professionals maintain a larger capital base.

A reasonable starting point for serious learning is around ₹1 lakh to ₹2 lakh.

Not because you will use all of it.

Because it provides a survival buffer.

---

## The Backup Capital Principle

Suppose you have ₹2 lakh.

You don't trade all ₹2 lakh.

Instead:

Trade 1 = ₹15,000

Trade 2 = ₹20,000

Trade 3 = ₹10,000

The remaining money stays as reserve.

This reserve protects you during losing streaks.

Professional traders focus on survival first.

Profit comes later.

---

## Realistic Returns vs Social Media Returns

Many Instagram and Telegram channels show:

"Turned ₹10,000 into ₹1 lakh in 3 days."

Possible?

Yes.

Repeatable?

Almost never.

Let's compare realistic benchmarks.

### Passive Investing

Expected Return:

15%–18% annually

Example:

₹1,00,000 invested

After one year:

₹1,15,000–₹1,18,000

This is realistic and sustainable.

---

### Active Trading

Expected Return:

Around 30% annually for a highly skilled trader.

Example:

Capital = ₹2 lakh

Annual Profit:

30% of ₹2 lakh = ₹60,000

Monthly Average:

₹5,000

This sounds boring.

But this is what professional consistency looks like.

---

## Why Most Traders Lose Money

Imagine 100 people enter the market.

Many think:

"I'll double my account."

"I'll quit my job in 6 months."

"I'll make ₹5,000 every day."

Because of these unrealistic expectations they:

* Overtrade
* Take huge risks
* Ignore stop losses
* Chase losses

Eventually they lose capital.

The problem isn't lack of intelligence.

The problem is unrealistic expectations.

---

## Learning From the World's Best

Let's compare expectations.

### Warren Buffett

One of the greatest investors ever.

Long-term returns:

Around 20% annually.

Yet he became one of the richest people in the world.

Why?

Consistency.

Compounding.

Time.

---

### Jim Simons

One of the greatest traders in history.

Generated around 66% annual returns through advanced mathematical systems.

Think about that.

The best trader in history achieved around 66%.

So when someone promises:

"100% every month guaranteed."

It's a warning sign.

---

## The Most Important Lesson

Don't ask:

"How much money can I make?"

Ask:

"What percentage return can I realistically generate while protecting my capital?"

Beginners focus on profits.

Professionals focus on percentages.

Experts focus on risk.

And the people who survive long enough eventually make the most money.

The first goal in the market is not becoming rich.

The first goal is staying in the game long enough for compounding to work.

This episode is arguably the most important in the entire series because it teaches the mindset shift from **"I need ₹5,000 today"** to **"Can I consistently generate 15–30% annually?"**. That single shift separates investing from gambling.
